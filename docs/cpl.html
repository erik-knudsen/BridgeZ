Computer Player Logic.

The computer is a fair player. The computer player is not as skillful as expert bridge players, but much better than novice players. A computer player bids naturally, plays cleverly, alerts and claims correctly just like an experienced player.


Bidding Rule Tables.

Bid algorithms are based on Bidding Rule Tables (Data Base of bidding systems). After the bid goes through the Bidding Rule Tables, the computer bids according to a built-in approach. The Bidding Rule Tables characterize the bidding system: five-card majors, weak twos, Jacoby transfers, etc. ZBridge allows you to choose among many bidding options.


Random generated hands.

Play algorithms are based on random generated hands. The computer randomly generates unknown hands under known conditions that are derived from information of the auction (HCP range, suit length, number of Aces). The computer regards the generated hands just as real hands and decides possible best plays using double dummy algorithms. Afterwards it chooses among these best plays using  information based on the defenders' signals. The computer iterates hand generation and makes decisions about the play. It evaluates the set of decisions derived from each simulation trial, and then makes the final play.

Built-In Approach of ZBridge Bidding.

ZBridge uses the Bidding Rule Tables at the early stage of the auction. After the bid goes through the Bidding Rule Tables, the computer will bid according to the built-in approach. Built-in approaches are as follows.


Non-Forcing and Limit.

New suit bids and rebids are usually non-forcing. Notrump bids and raises of partner's suit are limit bids.


Cue Bids.

Guarantees added strength to bid an opponent's suit or to bid a new suit after a trump fit is found, but does not guarantee any length of the bid suit.


Exception.

After a minor suit fit has been found, a new suit bid of three normally shows stoppers in the bid suit.


Doubles.

When the partner shows some strength, all doubles are for penalties. Otherwise, doubles against three or lower bids are for takeout and doubles against four or higher bids are for penalties.


Ace Asking Bids.

4NT is usually a Blackwood convention. 4C after 1NT or 2NT opening bids is Gerber convention.


Hand Valuation of ZBridge.

ZBridge supports various types of natural bidding systems. These systems are based on the common hand valuation and approach below.

High Card Points (HCP)
Ace=4     King=3     Queen=2     Jack=1

Distribution Points (DP) when a trump fit is found
1 point for each doubleton
2 points for each singleton
3 points for each void

Long Suit Points (LP)
If the hand contains many playing tricks as compared with its HCPs, add some points to the hand. For example, if the hand contains 16 HCP, add 1 point for each trick over 6 playing tricks.

Trump Fit Points (FP)
Add 1 point for each trump over 8-card fitting.

Total Points (TP)
TP=HCP+DP+LP+FP

Control Number
Ace=2     King=1

Required Points for a Game and Slam Contract

4 of a suit 26 combined points.
5 of a suit 29 combined points.
6 of a suit 33 combined points.
7 of a suit 37 combined points.
3NT 26 combined points.
6NT 33 combined points.
7NT 37 combined points.


Point Ranges
ZBridge usually bids according to the following point ranges.
Opener
   Minimum 12-15
   Intermediate 16-18 (game invitation zone)
   Strong 19-21 (game forcing zone)
   Very Strong 22+    (strong two opening zone)

Responder
   Minimum 6-9
   Intermediate 10-12 (game invitation zone)
   Strong 13-15 (game forcing zone)
   Very Strong 16+    (jump shift zone)

Note:

ZBridge jumps to a new suit with 16 or more points after the partner opens with one in a suit. This means that a jump shift is a slam invitation and is not a slam forcing.
